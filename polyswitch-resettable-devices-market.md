<p><p>PolySwitch resettable devices are commonly used in electrical circuits to protect them from damage caused by excessive current flow. They come in two main types: polymer and ceramic.</p><p>Polymer type PolySwitch resettable devices are made from a conductive polymer material that exhibits a positive temperature coefficient (PTC) behavior. This means that when the current flowing through the device exceeds a certain limit, the polymer material heats up and its resistance increases dramatically, limiting the current to a safe level. Polymer devices are known for their fast response time and high current carrying capabilities.</p><p>Ceramic type PolySwitch resettable devices, on the other hand, are made from ceramic materials. They utilize a bimetallic effect, where two different metals with different coefficients of expansion are bonded together. When the current exceeds a specific threshold, the device heats up, causing the bimetallic strip to bend and separate the electrical contacts, effectively interrupting the current flow. Ceramic devices are known for their reliability and long-term stability under high temperatures.</p><p>Both polymer and ceramic PolySwitch resettable devices serve the same purpose of protecting electrical circuits from overcurrent, but their underlying mechanisms and characteristics differ. The choice between these types depends on the specific requirements of the circuit, such as current rating, response time, and environmental conditions.</p></p>
